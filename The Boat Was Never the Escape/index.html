The Boat Was Never the Escape

There has been a boat somewhere in my mind for most of my adult life.

Sometimes it was very concrete — drawings, measurements, magazines stacked next to a bed. Sometimes it was only a dream image: a sailboat moving quietly, a sense of order, a place where things made sense again. Whenever life became cramped or incoherent, the boat returned.

For a long time I thought this meant I wanted a better boat. Bigger, stronger, more capable. If I could just get the right one, everything else would line up.

That turned out not to be true.

I have built a boat. I have sailed one. I have sold one. I have lived the fantasy version and the ordinary, disappointing version. What I learned is this: the boat is not the cure — it is the container.

A container for time.
A container for solitude.
A container for responsibility, and for freedom that doesn’t ask permission.

This site is not about how to sail around the world. There are already many people doing that very well. It is about what draws people toward that life in the first place — and what actually happens after the dream starts behaving like reality.

I am in the later part of my life. I am not chasing achievement, influence, or reinvention. I am interested in alignment: between how a life looks on the outside and how it feels on the inside. Between independence and connection. Between solitude and loneliness — which are not the same thing.

You’ll find here:

  • essays about boats, time, work, money, and meaning
  • reflections on autonomy without heroics
  • notes from a life that has tried things, abandoned others, and learned slowly

Some of this will resonate. Some of it won’t. That’s fine. This is not a program or a philosophy. It’s a record of thinking things through honestly, without pretending that freedom is simple or that solitude is always noble.

If you’re here because a boat keeps appearing in your own thoughts — literal or metaphorical — you’re not alone. And you’re not necessarily running away.

Sometimes you’re just looking for a structure strong enough to hold who you already are.
